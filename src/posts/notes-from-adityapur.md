Every MBA program has some version of a live project: a real company, a real problem, a semester to do something useful. At XLRI it is called BAS. When this one was floated, I volunteered. An MSME in the manufacturing belt of Jamshedpur, working on ESG and a digital production dashboard.

I had no operations background. No sustainability background. What I had was curiosity about what it would actually look like on the ground, and the good sense to recognise that the team around me did. Classmates who had worked in operations at Tata Steel and Hero. People who had reported on and advised around sustainability at Goldman. Faculty who are among the leading sustainability strategy thinkers in the country. I figured the honest move was to show up, stay close, and pay attention.

---

There is a version of this project that stays comfortably in the classroom. You pull records, build a baseline, run numbers, write a report, present it. That version exists and we did parts of it. The part that actually taught me something was the floor.

Walking through the plant on the first visit, you realise quickly that the competencies you brought in are not quite the competencies that are useful here. My Goldman background meant I knew how to read a compliance framework. My CS background meant I could think about data systems. Neither told me what I needed to know about how a factory actually breathes: what the real constraints are, where the knowledge lives, how decisions actually get made.

What transferred was not the technical skill. It was a disposition the technical work had trained: get comfortable in rooms where you do not know the most important things yet, ask the question that reveals you do not know, and stay long enough to actually learn something. The GS compliance work had taught me to look for the gap between process as documented and process as practiced. That instinct showed up on the floor immediately. The gap was everywhere, and it was interesting.

---

The dashboard is where the CS background actually showed up usefully.

The client, a 57-year-old manufacturing plant, wanted to skip phased implementation and go directly to app-based data entry integrated with a live dashboard. He had adjacent operations that already ran on full digital automation and saw no reason to spend a year getting there via Excel. He was right.

What would normally take weeks of scoping, I started prototyping the same day using Claude. Not to ship production code, but to get something visual and interactive in front of the client fast, so the conversation could move from what we planned to build to what he actually needed. That compression matters in a consulting context. A client responds to something real in a way they cannot respond to a slide. When you can put a working prototype in front of someone in an afternoon, the feedback loop tightens from weeks to hours, and you learn things about the actual problem that no amount of upfront specification would have surfaced.

This is the version of AI-assisted work I find genuinely interesting: not replacing the thinking, but collapsing the distance between an idea and something you can watch a person react to. The shop floor made that vivid. The test was immediate and concrete. Either the line manager can enter the data in thirty seconds at the end of a shift, or the whole thing fails. No abstraction available.

---

In [The Plate is Wobbling](/blog/the-plate-is-wobbling), I wrote about a day doing market immersion with a sales team for my Unilever internship, walking through neighbourhood shops, watching a distribution rep operate from memory and instinct, and noticing the gap between how a tool is designed and how it is used on the ground. The MSME project has the same texture.

Both are variations of the same practice: showing up somewhere you do not fully understand, staying past the surface, and paying attention to the things that do not fit the framework you brought in. In both cases, the person on the ground had knowledge that no briefing document had captured. The gap between the model and the room is not a failure of the model. It is where the real learning is.

---

Sustainability as a concept is easy to be cynical about. In a Tier 2 manufacturing context it lands differently. Large OEMs are building out platforms to track emissions across their vendor ecosystems. For a small plant with deep revenue concentration in one client, ESG compliance is not a values question. It is a survival question. The work is not about convincing anyone that sustainability matters. It is about helping a plant become legible to a reporting infrastructure that is being built around it whether it is ready or not.

The interesting ethical question lurking underneath all of this is whether making something measurable and reported is a meaningful step toward actually improving it, or a substitute for that. I do not have a clean answer. I think it is both, simultaneously, and that the honest thing is to say so rather than collapse it into either the cynical reading or the idealistic one.

---

In [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play), I wrote that deep preparation in the fundamentals is what makes wandering in new domains rich rather than shallow. What I keep finding in the MBA is the corollary: you do not always need to be the one with the deep preparation. Sometimes the move is to be genuinely curious in a room full of people who have it, contribute where your specific foundations are useful, and stay out of the way when they are not.

The wobbling plate was interesting to Feynman because he had the physics underneath him. I did not have the operations or the sustainability. But I had classmates who did, faculty who did, and enough of a technical foundation to be useful on the dashboard and the data side. The team is the preparation. The plate is still wobbling.
