In my last post, I wrote about Feynman's wobbling plate and the idea that preparation gives you the surplus to be curious. This week, I got to test that theory in the most literal way possible: walking through neighbourhood shops with a sales rep in a city I'd never been to, watching a distribution system I'd never thought about actually work.

As part of my MBA internship, I spent a day doing a market immersion with the sales team in the field. No slides. No models. Just showing up at shops, watching transactions happen, and trying to understand why.

Coming from compliance and engineering at Goldman, where the work is careful, documented, and mostly invisible to the end customer, this was a different kind of system entirely. The rep I shadowed knew each shopkeeper personally. He knew what had moved last week, what was gathering dust, and how to pitch something new without any prep material in hand. The "data" was in his head, built from hundreds of these visits.

What made it extra interesting was watching technology sit inside that world. There are digital tools built for this salesforce, handling order placement, surveys, new product launches, targeted promotions. As someone who has spent time thinking about distributed systems, seeing these tools deployed across thousands of reps and retailers gave me a concrete sense of what scale means outside of a compliance dashboard. But the sharper observation was the gap between how a tool is designed and how it is used on the ground. Frontline reps had instincts about customer behaviour that no product specification had captured.

---

I kept thinking about what a strange week this actually was. A tech internship inside an FMCG company. A market visit to understand the bread and butter of a business I'm approaching entirely from the technology side. Meanwhile, fresh off writing the CFA, a live consulting project advising an MSME on the intersection of sustainability and technology, and a CS and AI research background underneath all of it. On paper it looks scattered. In practice it is starting to feel like the point.

This is what I think business school might actually be, at its best: a structured attempt to become a polymath. Not someone who knows a little about everything, but someone building enough depth in enough domains that the connections between them start to feel generative rather than accidental. The Feynman essay I wrote last week was about how preparation gives you the freedom to play. I think the MBA is the preparation. All of it at once, spinning simultaneously.

---

In "Prepared to Play," I wrote about Osterweil's freedom to try on identities as one of the conditions for genuine learning. A day in the field is exactly that: you are not the analyst, not the MBA student, not the person with frameworks. You are just someone trying to keep up and pay attention.

The plate is wobbling. I'm still working out the equation.
