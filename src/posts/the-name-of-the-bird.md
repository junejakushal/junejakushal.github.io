Feynman's father taught him early not to confuse knowing the name of something with knowing the thing itself. On their walks together, he would point to a bird and say: that is a brown-throated thrush. In Portuguese it is this word. In Italian, another. In Japanese, a third. Now you know the name in every language in the world. You still know nothing about the bird.

What you learn about the bird is what it does. How it moves. What it eats. Why it pecks the way it does. The name is the filing system. The behavior is the knowledge.

---

I spent the first week of my Unilever internship in Mumbai, in rooms full of marketing heads for divisions that are individually larger than most companies. Dove. Surf Excel. Taj Mahal. Clinic Plus. Lakme. Brands that have been in Indian households longer than some of the people managing them have been alive.

The induction was five days. By the end of it I had learned more about marketing than in three trimesters at XL. I do not mean this as a criticism of XL. I mean the induction was five days inside a machine that actually runs, and a classroom is necessarily a model of one.

---

The thing that stayed with me most was what they do with the idea of a Target Group.

The Kotlerian version, which is what most of us learn first, carves up the population by demographic: age, gender, income, occupation. The implicit picture is something like a 35-year-old male who commutes to work and buys things. You know the name of the bird. You have the demographic label. You still know nothing about the person.

What HUL does instead is closer to asking what the bird actually does. They think about TGs as communities. Not segments defined by who people are on paper, but clusters defined by how they actually move through the world. What they watch. What they share. What they care about. People who are together in the data for a reason that the data then lets you discover.

---

I have seen this problem from the other side.

At IIIT-Delhi I worked in the LCS2 lab on a project that clustered users based on their social media interactions and then analyzed the latent representations of those clusters to understand them. The thing we kept finding was that the latent space was not just a classification tool. It was an explanation. You did not just learn that these users belonged together. You learned, from the structure of the space, something about why. The data told the story rather than the other way around.

Sitting in those Mumbai presentations, watching their dashboards render, I kept thinking about the same geometry. Trend analysis. Sentiment analysis. Anomaly detection. Random forests. Deep learning. Every technique I had spent four years learning at undergraduate level showed up in production on someone's screen, doing actual work for actual decisions. That feeling of recognition is its own kind of education.

---

HUL's Winning In Many Indias strategy is built on this logic. If the country is not one market but many overlapping communities, and if those communities are legible through behavior rather than through demographic proxies, then in principle you can reach people at a granularity the Kotler model cannot access. The label tells you nothing about which India a person lives in. The community tells you something closer to the truth.

None of this is simple. The brands that command the kirana shelf, the stock market, and the online store are still losing sleep over D2C challengers who built their audiences in Instagram comment sections, over new brands that move faster than a portfolio review cycle, over the fact that when someone asks an LLM what shampoo to buy, the answer increasingly does not contain a brand name at all. The storytelling moat that took fifty years to build is being approached from a direction that nobody fully mapped.

The decisions I watched being made are not for next quarter. They are for the next ten years.

---

I came in expecting a product management project connecting business with technology. I was handed a digital marketing project doing data mining instead.

After Los Alamos, Feynman arrived at Cornell burned out. He had spent years on the most consequential physics project in history and felt he had nothing left. He was sitting in the campus cafeteria when someone threw a plate in the air. He watched the Cornell medallion wobble as the plate spun and noticed something: the wobble and the spin were not at the same rate. He wanted to know the ratio. There was no reason to work it out. It was not important. He did it anyway, just because the plate was interesting.

That equation, worked out for nothing, for the pleasure of following a wobble no one had asked him to follow, became the work that led to QED. Which became the Nobel Prize.

I do not know yet what the data mining project will become. The question underneath it is real: how do you do insighting at scale when the volume of signal makes manual synthesis impossible and the stakes require more than automated summarisation? The agentic version of this does not fully exist yet. Not here. Not anywhere I can point to clearly.

But I was handed the plate. I am watching it spin.
