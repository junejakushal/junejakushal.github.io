There is a Richard Feynman story I keep returning to.

After Los Alamos, Feynman fell into a depression. The bomb had been built. His wife had died. Physics, which had once felt electric, now felt like obligation. He was at Cornell, supposedly one of the best physicists in the world, and he couldn't bring himself to care about any of it.

Then one day in the cafeteria, someone tossed a dinner plate into the air. Feynman watched it wobble. He noticed that the plate's spin and its wobble were out of sync — the wobble was twice as fast as the rotation — and he found himself wondering *why*.

Not because anyone asked him to. Not because it would lead anywhere. Just because it was strange and he wanted to know.

He worked out the equation. Then another. Then he showed it to Hans Bethe, who asked what it was good for. Feynman said: nothing. "It's just fun." Within a year, that playful detour had fed directly into his work on quantum electrodynamics. A Nobel Prize would follow.

The wobbling plate wasn't the point. The point was that play — genuine, unobligated, aimless curiosity — brought him back to the thing he was supposed to be serious about.

---

I have a fun fact I give at introductions: *Overprepare. Always. You can't wing it and assume someone else will do it for you.*

I believe this. I have lived by it. At Goldman Sachs, in compliance and engineering, the job was fundamentally about not being caught off guard — knowing the regulation before the regulator quoted it at you, knowing the failure mode before it became a failure. Preparation was the product.

But I've started to think that overpreparing and playing are not opposites. They might be, in a strange way, the same gesture.

Scot Osterweil, a researcher at MIT's Education Arcade, describes four freedoms that define genuine play: the freedom to experiment, the freedom to fail, the freedom to try on identities, and the freedom to exert effort on your own terms. What strikes me about this list is how much it sounds like the conditions for learning at its best — not classroom learning, but the kind that sticks.

And here is what I've noticed: the freedom to experiment and fail is much easier to claim when you have a foundation underneath you.

When I worked on climate modeling at IIIT-Delhi — trying to coax deep learning into predicting monsoon patterns — I was a CS student playing in Earth sciences, a domain I had no business being in. I failed constantly. But the failures felt like play rather than disaster because I had enough of a technical foundation to know *why* I was failing. The CS was the net. The climate modeling was the tightrope.

At Goldman, I understood financial systems well enough to ask strange questions about them — why this model, why this approximation, what breaks first. The preparation gave me the surplus to be curious.

---

I am now an MBA student, which means I am, professionally, a beginner again. Finance I know. Technology I know. But strategy, marketing, operations, organizational behavior — these are new territories. So is Unilever, which I'll be joining this summer: consumer goods, a category I've observed only as a consumer.

The honest feeling is discomfort. The overpreparer in me wants to have already mastered things before I arrive in them. But I've been trying to hold Osterweil's freedoms in mind — especially the freedom to try on identities. To be, for a while, someone who doesn't know. To let the wobbling plate be interesting before asking what it's good for.

There's a particular freedom in being a student that professionals lose and rarely reclaim deliberately. You are *expected* to be confused. Questions are the currency. You can try an idea on for a semester and discard it. Business school, at its best, is a structured space for exactly the kind of play Osterweil is describing — which is probably why the best MBAs I've met treat it that way, and the miserable ones treat it like a credential-maximization exercise.

---

I don't think the lesson here is *be more playful* in some vague, motivational-poster sense. Feynman was not a dilettante. He prepared obsessively. He knew quantum mechanics as well as anyone alive. The wobbling plate wasn't a random whim — it was a trained intuition suddenly freed from obligation.

The lesson, if there is one, is about sequence. You prepare deeply in the fundamentals. Then you let yourself wander in the applications. The depth is what makes the wandering rich rather than shallow.

I am trying to build the kind of foundations — in technology, in finance, in how organizations actually work — that will let me play seriously in whatever comes next. The aspiration to be a polymath is not about being superficially curious about everything. It is about being deep enough in enough things that the connections between them start to feel like play.

The plate wobbles. I want to keep noticing that.
