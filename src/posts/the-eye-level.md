Walk into a kirana store. Notice what is at eye level. Not the cheapest item. Not the most popular. The highest margin item, often from competing brands sitting side by side. The owner knows this without a dashboard. No A/B test. Forty years of watching what gets picked up.

Walk into a D-Mart. The logic shifts. Volume and price. The consumer has planned this trip, has a basket already forming in their head, knows roughly what they will spend. Placement still matters but the occasion is different.

Open Blinkit. Eye level is now the first scroll position. The margin logic is still there, just embedded in a ranking algorithm. No one decided it the way the kirana owner decided it. It was optimized into existence.

Open Amazon. Brand search weakens. Discovery happens through reviews, sponsored positions, and increasingly through AI-generated recommendations that may not mention the brand name at all.

Four channels. Four entirely different businesses. One company managing all of them, with different SKUs, different price architectures, different messages, and different definitions of winning for each.

---

Sachets are not sold on quick commerce. This sounds like a small fact. It is actually a complete portrait of a consumer.

The sachet exists because the consumer does not have the cash flow to buy the full bottle, does not have the storage to justify it, and is making a purchase decision in the moment at the counter of a kirana. The quick commerce consumer has a smartphone, a delivery address, a saved payment method, and a basket that justifies a minimum order value. These are not the same person. Treating them as the same channel because both move goods quickly is a category error that compounds at scale.

And everyone, at every level of every channel, wants to build their own D2C pipeline now. Not primarily for margin, though that is the stated reason. For the data. The kirana owner knows which sachets move on which days of the week by instinct. The brand manager wants that knowledge systematized, owned, and predictive. D2C gives you the consumer relationship directly. No intermediary holding the data. No retailer deciding what goes at eye level. The pipe from factory to consumer becomes a learning system instead of a distribution channel.

---

HUL manages tea and coffee and skin and hair and oral care and lip care and home hygiene. Brands that have been in Indian households longer than most of the people managing them have been alive. Brands with awareness numbers that most companies would restructure themselves around. And they are still losing sleep over D2C challengers who started in Instagram comment sections, over new entrants that move faster than a portfolio review cycle, over the fact that when someone asks an LLM what moisturiser to buy, the output increasingly does not contain a brand name.

The decisions I am watching made in rooms in Mumbai and Bangalore are not calibrated to the next quarter. They are calibrated to the next decade.

---

I came in to Unilever through a different door entirely, thinking the internship would be about connecting technology to business operations.

Instead I was talking to brand managers and media planners about offtake numbers, on-shelf availability, share of voice, return on investment for digital spend. Metrics I had only seen in cleaned-up case studies, now live, contested, and consequential.

The agentic AI work is real and it is moving fast inside the organisation. Priya Nair and Fernando Fernandez have been explicit about the AI-first direction. But the people building agentic systems have to understand what a brand manager actually does before they can automate any part of it. What decisions they make on what cadence. What signal they are actually responding to when they shift spend between channels. You cannot MECE-ify a workflow you do not understand. Think MECE, speak MECE, act MECE: easy to say, only possible after you have mapped the thing you are trying to be mutually exclusive and collectively exhaustive about.

The learning curve is steep. It is also the most interesting three weeks I have had.

---

In [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play) I wrote about deep preparation as the thing that makes wandering in new domains rich rather than shallow. I did not know at the time what domain I was preparing for.

Clustering users by social media interaction at LCS2 in IIIT-Delhi. Training petabyte-scale compliance models at Goldman that had to be regularised hard enough to generalise without overfitting to historical patterns. Building climate models at IIT-Delhi where the signal was buried in noise and the model had to learn to be appropriately uncertain. The CFA curriculum sitting half-used but giving me enough financial language to follow a margin conversation without stopping to ask what EBITDA means. The MBA frameworks, sometimes too clean for the ground but useful as scaffolding.

None of those were FMCG. None of them were marketing. All of them are now useful in the same room at the same time.

---

Feynman once described his method: keep a dozen of your favourite problems present in your mind, dormant but loaded. Every time you encounter a new result, a new technique, a new way of seeing something, test it against all twelve. Most of the time nothing happens. Occasionally, a hit. And when the hit comes, people call it genius. It is not genius. It is just having kept the problems present and stayed on the lookout.

I have not been working twelve problems. I have been working one: how does understanding at one level of abstraction help or fail you at another? How does the model you built in the lab translate to the decision someone makes in a kirana at six in the evening?

Three weeks into HUL, with Karpathy's AI research framing on one screen and offtake dashboards on the other, I think I am finally close enough to the question to start finding out.
