The story goes like this. Feynman was at Caltech when the offer came from Cornell. Serious money. Serious resources. A formal appointment at a serious institution. By every conventional metric, the kind of offer you do not turn down.

He turned it down.

What changed his mind, as he tells it, was something that happened in the pit — the informal lounge at Caltech's physics building where people gathered between seminars and after talks. A biology student stopped him and started describing an experiment. Not a physics experiment. Biology. Feynman had not asked about it. It was not on his schedule. The student was just excited and Feynman happened to be there.

That was it. That was the thing Cornell could not put in the offer letter: what a random Tuesday afternoon felt like when the ambient intellectual density of the place was high enough to make accidental contact with you.

He wrote the letter. He stayed.

---

I joined CRISP this year. For those outside XLRI: CRISP runs placements. Nineteen people, spread across two cities, coordinating the logistics of 600 students' biggest professional day. Day Zero. The calls that change trajectories.

By the grammar of MBA achievement-listing, this is a strange choice. There is no fund size to report. No footfall number. No revenue line. A consulting project has deliverables. A finance committee has a portfolio. CRISP has outcomes that are mostly invisible until the day they are not, and then they belong to someone else anyway.

I knew this when I signed up. I signed up anyway.

---

The honest version of why starts with what everything I have built before this actually was.

Goldman systems. Climate models. The sustainability dashboard in Adityapur. The architecture I put together for the MSME, which I wrote about in [The Pipe and the Pit](/blog/the-pipe-and-the-pit). All of it was, at some level, the same move: understand the system, design the thing, let the thing run. The satisfaction was in the design. Once it worked, you could step away.

That is increasingly delegable. Not just to a junior engineer or a contractor — to an agent. The parts of my work that involve pattern recognition, structured reasoning, and translating requirements into a running system are exactly the parts that AI handles well and is getting better at handling. I know this because I use these tools every day and I watch what they can do.

CRISP is not delegable. Not to a system, not to an agent, not to a process.

You cannot stub out a recruiter's hesitation on a call at 11pm. You cannot mock a teammate's anxiety the night before Day Zero. You cannot unit-test a negotiation where the other side is a person with their own pressures and constraints and read of the situation. The medium is the people, which means the only way to learn this is to be in the room. There is no architecture. There is no thing that runs.

This is not "I want to develop soft skills." That framing undersells it. This is a deliberate choice to work in a domain where my default toolkit — the one I have been sharpening since IIIT-Delhi, the one I carried through Goldman and climate research and every project since — simply does not reach. I am going somewhere the preparation does not cover.

---

In [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play), I wrote that deep preparation is what makes genuine curiosity possible. The foundation is what gives you the surplus to wander. In [The Messy Reactor](/blog/the-messy-reactor), I wrote about choosing the inelegant setup over the proper lab because the mess is where the real questions are still being asked.

CRISP is both of those things pushed further than I have gone before. It is as inelegant as it gets: no clean data, no audited inputs, no system to hand off to. Just nineteen people, a deadline, and 600 students whose professional lives are partly in the coordination.

And it is a domain where the preparation I have does not straightforwardly apply. The Goldman compliance instinct — find the gap between the process as documented and the process as practiced — that might transfer. The habit of staying in rooms where I do not know the most important things yet, which I wrote about in [Notes from Adityapur](/blog/notes-from-adityapur), that will definitely transfer. But the core of the work is irreducibly human, which means I am starting close to zero.

---

Back to Feynman and the pit.

The biology student did not teach Feynman biology. He did not give Feynman anything formally transferable. What he did was remind Feynman that the right environment produces collisions you did not plan for, and that those collisions are what the formal offer letter cannot measure.

Cornell was optimising along one axis. Feynman was optimising along a different one entirely: the quality of what an ordinary afternoon produces when the people around you are working seriously on hard things.

Nineteen people across two cities, under genuine pressure, with real stakes and real consequences — that is the pit. Not because of what the role says on paper. Because of who is in the room and what is at stake when it matters.

I wrote the letter. I stayed.
