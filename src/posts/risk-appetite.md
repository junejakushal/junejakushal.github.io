For a Financial Management course at XLRI, our team got time with the CFO office of JSW Steel and separately with the corporate finance team at KEI Industries. Two calls, same week, same course, same questions roughly oriented around capital structure and risk.

Both teams, at different points, used the phrase "risk appetite."

KEI used it to explain why they raised equity for a major greenfield plant despite having a near-zero debt balance sheet and a top credit rating. Cheap debt was available. They chose not to use it. The philosophy, as I understood it, is that the balance sheet stays clean so that when the next opportunity comes, nothing is encumbered. Zero debt is not just a financial position. It is a strategic posture.

JSW used it to explain why debt is not something to be minimised but something to be deployed. A capital-intensive steel business cannot grow without leverage. The discipline is not in avoiding debt but in staying within covenants, stress-testing at the project level, and making sure each asset services its own obligations. At one point, someone on the call referenced Damodaran directly, the same Damodaran whose equity risk premium tables we pull up in class, whose blog posts we read on weekends. Hearing his name in a live CFO conversation landed differently than it does on a problem set.

Same phrase. Opposite balance sheets. Both internally consistent.

---

This is the thing about theory that the classroom version of finance cannot quite convey. Damodaran's framework, the CFA curriculum's treatment of capital structure, Modigliani-Miller, the trade-off theory, the pecking order hypothesis: these are not competing answers to a single question. They are lenses, and which one you reach for depends on what kind of business you are sitting inside, what your covenant obligations look like, what your customer concentration is, how capital-intensive your industry is, and how much of your growth is already funded by internal accruals versus external markets.

Both companies are citing the same underlying theory to arrive at structurally opposite conclusions, and both are right to do so. That is not a contradiction. That is what it means for a framework to be a tool rather than a rule.

I have been studying for the CFA alongside the MBA. The curriculum is thorough on the mechanics. What the two conversations gave me was something the curriculum cannot: the weight of consequence. An internal buffer maintained below a covenant ceiling is not a textbook exercise. That buffer is what stands between a company and a significant event in a down cycle. A clean balance sheet cited as a door-opener with international infrastructure clients is not a lecture slide. It is a sales argument built on a financial position. The theory is the preparation. The conversation is where it wobbles.

---

Knowing the Damodaran methodology meant I could follow the cost of capital reasoning in real time rather than spending mental energy on the mechanics. Knowing the CFA treatment of natural hedges meant I could ask a follow-up about where the real commodity exposure actually sits rather than asking what a natural hedge is. The preparation does not make you an expert in the room. It makes you a better listener.

This is the version of that observation I keep finding in the MBA, and I wrote about it first in [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play): the surplus shows up not as answers but as the ability to stay in the conversation longer.

---

I want to try to describe what a single week in this MBA can look like.

One day I am on a call with a CFO team asking about covenant discipline and stress testing for a greenfield project worth tens of thousands of crores. Another day I am doing a market visit with a sales rep for my Unilever internship, watching how a distribution system operates at the neighbourhood shop level, noticing the gap between how a tool is designed and how it is used on the ground, which I wrote about in [The Plate is Wobbling](/blog/the-plate-is-wobbling). Another day I am on a factory floor in Jamshedpur prototyping a sustainability dashboard and trying to figure out if a line manager can enter emissions data in thirty seconds at the end of a shift. And underneath all of it, a CFA curriculum, a climate and deep learning research background from IIIT-Delhi, two years in Goldman's compliance and engineering division.

On paper this looks scattered. In practice I think it is the point.

Feynman's plates were all spinning simultaneously. The physics of the wobble was the same whether the plate was the rotating dinner plate in the Cornell cafeteria or the quantum electrodynamics problem he had been avoiding. The motion connects if you are paying attention to the motion rather than the specific plate.

The motion I keep noticing: the gap between the model and the room. The CFA tells you how a natural hedge works. The CFO tells you what sitting on months of copper inventory actually feels like when prices move. The classroom teaches capital allocation frameworks. The call teaches you what the CFO team actually loses sleep over, which is rarely what the framework says it should be. Damodaran tells you how to think about risk. The conversation tells you that risk appetite is the same phrase meaning almost opposite things at two different companies, and both of them are right.

---

There is a version of the MBA where you optimise for the credential. You do the courses, hit the grades, line up the internship. That version is fine. It is not what I am here for.

The thing I keep reaching for is closer to what serious play actually looks like in practice: using the preparation as fuel rather than armour, staying curious in rooms where the stakes are real, letting the wobble be interesting before trying to solve it. The CFA is fuel. The Damodaran reading is fuel. The Goldman years are fuel. All of it available in the room, not as a script but as a surplus.

The plates are spinning. The wobble is the interesting part.
