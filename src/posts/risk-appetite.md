For a Financial Management course at XLRI, our team got time with the CFO office of JSW Steel and separately with the Senior VP of Corporate Finance at KEI Industries. Two calls, same week, same course, same questions roughly oriented around capital structure and risk.

Both teams, at different points, used the phrase "risk appetite."

KEI used it to explain why they raised equity for a major greenfield plant despite having a near-zero debt balance sheet and an AA+ credit rating. Cheap debt was available. They chose not to use it. The philosophy, as I understood it, was that the balance sheet stays clean so that when the next opportunity comes, nothing is encumbered. Zero debt is not just a financial position. It is a strategic posture.

JSW used it to explain why debt is not something to be minimised but something to be deployed. A capital-intensive steel business cannot grow without leverage. The discipline is not in avoiding debt but in staying within covenants, stress-testing at the project level, and making sure each asset services its own obligations. At some point in the conversation, one of the team members referenced Damodaran directly, the same Damodaran whose equity risk premium tables we pull up in class, whose blog posts we read on weekends. Hearing his name in a live CFO conversation landed differently than it does in a case study.

Same phrase. Opposite balance sheets. Both internally consistent.

---

This is the thing about theory that the classroom version of finance cannot quite convey. Damodaran's framework, the CFA curriculum's treatment of capital structure, Modigliani-Miller, the trade-off theory, the pecking order hypothesis: these are not competing answers to a single question. They are lenses, and which one you reach for depends on what kind of business you are sitting inside, what your covenant obligations look like, what your customer concentration is, how capital-intensive your industry is, and how much of your growth is already funded by internal accruals versus external markets.

KEI's logic is coherent within KEI's context. JSW's logic is coherent within JSW's context. Neither is wrong. The interesting thing is that both are citing the same underlying theory to arrive at structurally opposite conclusions, and both are right to do so. That is not a contradiction. That is what it means for a framework to be a tool rather than a rule.

I have been studying for the CFA alongside the MBA. The Level 1 curriculum is thorough on the mechanics. What the two CFO conversations gave me was something the curriculum cannot: the weight of consequence. When Vaibhav at JSW talks about maintaining an internal buffer of 1x below the covenant ceiling, that is not a textbook exercise. That buffer is what stands between the company and a significant event in a down cycle. When Kishore Kunal at KEI talks about a clean balance sheet being a door-opener with international clients like National Grid UK, that is not a lecture slide. That is a sales argument built on a financial position.

The theory is the preparation. The conversation is where it wobbles.

---

I wrote in [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play) that deep preparation gives you the surplus to be curious. What I keep finding is that the surplus shows up in unexpected places. Knowing the Damodaran methodology meant I could follow JSW's cost of capital reasoning in real time rather than spending mental energy on the mechanics. Knowing the CFA treatment of natural hedges meant that when KEI walked through their copper inventory strategy, I could ask a follow-up about where the real exposure actually sits rather than asking what a natural hedge is. The preparation does not make you an expert. It makes you a better listener.

---

I want to try to describe what a week in this MBA actually looks like, because I think it is relevant to what I am trying to work out.

One day I am on a call with the CFO office of a company that produces 34 million tons of steel a year, asking about covenant discipline and stress testing methodology for a 33,000 crore greenfield project. Another day I am doing a market visit with a sales rep for my Unilever internship, watching how a distribution system operates at the neighbourhood shop level, thinking about the gap between how a tool is designed and how it is used on the ground, which I wrote about in [The Plate is Wobbling](/blog/the-plate-is-wobbling). Another day I am on the factory floor of an MSME in Jamshedpur, prototyping a sustainability dashboard with Claude and trying to figure out if a line manager can enter emissions data in thirty seconds at the end of a shift, which I wrote about in [Notes from Adityapur](/blog/notes-from-adityapur). And somewhere underneath all of this, the CFA curriculum, a climate and deep learning research background from IIIT-Delhi, two years in Goldman's compliance and engineering division.

On paper this looks scattered. In practice I think it is the point.

Feynman's plates were all spinning simultaneously. The physics of the wobble was the same whether the plate was the rotating dinner plate in the Cornell cafeteria or the quantum electrodynamics problem he had been avoiding. The motion connects if you are paying attention to the motion rather than the specific plate.

The motion I keep noticing across all of these: the gap between the model and the room. The CFA tells you how a natural hedge works. The CFO tells you what sitting on three months of copper inventory actually feels like when prices move. The MBA classroom tells you about capital allocation frameworks. The JSW call tells you that the 30 percent equity component timing is what the CFO team actually loses sleep over, not the 70 percent debt, because the debt markets are predictable and internal accruals are the variable. The Damodaran blog tells you how to think about risk. The conversation tells you that risk appetite is the same phrase being used by two different companies to mean almost opposite things, and both of them are correct.

---

There is a version of the MBA where you optimise for the credential. You do the courses, you hit the grades, you line up the internship. That version is fine. It is not what I am here for.

What I am here for is closer to what Chelsea Sierra Voss describes when she writes about serious play, which I cited in [Prepared to Play](/blog/prepared-to-play): pursuing genuinely hard things with the spirit of genuine curiosity, using the preparation as fuel rather than armour. The CFA is fuel. The Damodaran reading is fuel. The Goldman compliance years are fuel. The DL and climate research is fuel. All of it available in the room, not as a script but as a surplus.

The plates are spinning. The wobble is the interesting part.
